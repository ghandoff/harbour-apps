TL;DR — my three picks

Out of 13 categories of multi-portal homepage design I looked at, three are the strongest fit for what you're trying to do:

🥇 Fog-of-war exploration over an illustrated harbour map (NEW, added 19 May) — top-down illustrated harbour, but each pier is initially dim and "lights up" as the visitor scrolls or hovers. Combines categories 1 and 13. Closest mental model: Roll20's "Explorer Mode" + Animal Crossing's island reveal. Strongest because it leans all the way into the metaphor — you don't just see the harbour, you walk the harbour. EFFORT: HIGH SVG + scroll-tied JS, but the scroll engine itself is small.

🥈 Horizontal "boardwalk" rails per pier — each pier becomes a single-row Netflix-style horizontal scroll along a stylised wooden planking band, with the pier label in oversized lowercase running parallel to the boardwalk. Strongest because it's the cheapest visual layer that feels like piers. EFFORT: MEDIUM Pure CSS.

🥉 Bento grid per pier with strong inter-pier water dividers — keep the 2-col grid inside each pier but separate piers with full-bleed wave/water dividers, and give each pier a distinct background tint. Ships in 4 hours and looks intentional. EFFORT: LOW

If you want me to be opinionated given the 10-day window: ship #2 (boardwalks) for May 28, then layer #1+#13 (fog-of-war map) on top in mid-June as a phase-two enhancement. The boardwalks give 80% of the metaphor at 20% of the production cost this week; the fog-of-war map is the version that wins design awards, but needs more time than we have before PRME lands.

How to use this doc

  • 13 categories ordered roughly from highest-to-lowest fit for the harbour metaphor (with the new fog-of-war pattern slotted last in the list but ranked #1 in picks).
  • Each has 2-4 concrete examples with URLs you can click.
  • "Harbour fit" rating: ★★★ strong, ★★ usable with modification, ★ poor fit.
  • "Effort" tells you what you'd need to build/commission.

1. Spatial / illustrated map navigation ★★★

The reader sees the place before navigating into any specific room. Each clickable region is a literal location on an illustrated map. This is the most literal expression of the harbour metaphor.

Examples

  • Walt Disney World theme park maps — the canonical example. Four parks shown as illustrated icons; hover or tap reveals attractions. The map establishes territory before the menu becomes a list.
  • Animal Crossing: New Horizons island map — top-down isometric, building-by-building. Sets the rule that exploration is a verb.
  • Rod Hunt illustrated maps — commercial illustrator who has done this for parks, campuses, and brand portals for 30 years. Useful as a reference style if we commission custom work.
  • Icograms Designer — DIY isometric map builder if we want to prototype before commissioning. Stock icon library + simple drag-drop.

Why it could work for the harbour

The metaphor is waiting to be made literal. A top-down view of the harbour with three piers visibly jutting into water (plus a drydock area on land) reads instantly. Click the pier to scroll to the section, or click an individual game tile on the pier to launch the app.

Why it might not

Production cost. A good illustrated harbour map takes a real illustrator 1-3 weeks. A bad one undermines the brand. AI-generated alternatives often look generic — but a tasteful flat/isometric SVG done in your existing palette could land in 2-3 days.

Effort

  • Stock-style isometric (1-2 days): Use icograms.com or Vecteezy to source pier/water/boardwalk elements, compose in Figma, export SVG.
  • Custom illustration (1-3 weeks): Commission an illustrator. £400-£1,500 depending on detail.
  • Generative + hand-cleanup (3-5 days): Midjourney/Sora draft, illustrator cleanup to match your palette. Risky on brand consistency.

2. Horizontal "rails" / Netflix rows ★★★

A single row per category that scrolls horizontally. Hugely effective at communicating "there are many things here, organised by group" without needing a single decision from the visitor upfront.

Examples

  • Netflix homepage — the canonical pattern. Each row is a category; horizontal scroll within row, vertical scroll between rows.
  • Spotify Home — same pattern, mood-keyed rather than personalised.
  • Apple TV+ landing — softer styling but the same row architecture, with hero cards that span 2-3 row positions for editorial emphasis.
  • Disney+ profile homepage — explicit branded "shelves" for Pixar, Marvel, Star Wars — each feels like a distinct sub-property within one site.

How it would translate to harbour

  • Pier A becomes a horizontal rail labelled "pier a — leadership" with each Wave-1 app as a card scrolling sideways.
  • Pier B is the next rail down.
  • CastParade between Pier B and Pier C stays as decorative tissue.
  • Pier C is a special hybrid (waitlist hero + 3 character preview cards, treated as a "coming soon" shelf).
  • Drydock is a denser final rail of low-opacity tiles.

Harbour-specific styling lever

Each "rail" is rendered on a stylised wooden boardwalk — a CSS-only horizontal band with subtle plank texture (linear gradients perpendicular to scroll direction). The pier label sits at the start of the rail in oversized lowercase type that reads parallel to the boards. The water (page background) lives between rails.

Why it works for piers specifically

A pier IS a horizontal structure. A vertical 2-column grid actively fights the metaphor. A horizontal rail mirrors the physical shape.

Why it might not

Horizontal scroll is sometimes accessibility-hostile on touchscreens (especially when nested inside vertical scroll). Mitigation: always show "next" / "previous" arrow buttons + keyboard navigation + ensure each card is reachable without scroll (e.g., snap to grid on smaller screens, fall back to wrap on the smallest).

Effort

EFFORT: MEDIUM No custom illustrations. ~2-3 days of CSS work.

3. Bento grid (modular tile mosaic) ★★★

Asymmetric grid of differently-sized tiles. Each tile is roughly self-contained but the composition of varied sizes creates visual hierarchy without any explicit titles or sections.

Examples

  • Apple M-series Mac mini page — the canonical 2023+ bento.
  • Linear features page — bento layout where every tile is a product capability.
  • Notion homepage — uses bentos for template previews mixed with testimonials and features.
  • Bentogrids.com — gallery of current bento implementations.

How it would translate to harbour

Each pier becomes its own bento section. Pier A is a 2x2 or 3x2 bento with one "hero" tile (e.g., vault) sized larger than the rest. Within each pier, the bento creates visual interest the current uniform 2-col grid lacks. Piers separated by full-bleed wave/water graphics.

Why it works

  • 67% of SaaS top-100 homepages on ProductHunt now use bento. It's the current vernacular.
  • 47% longer dwell time vs. uniform grids (per Senorit's 2026 analysis).
  • It lets you give editorial emphasis to the most important app per pier without a separate "featured" callout.

Why it might not

Bentos can feel generic if all four piers look the same. Mitigation: give each pier a distinct tonal background + a unique tile-size composition so the shape of each pier reads differently. Pier A heavy on landscape tiles, Pier B more square, Pier C tall (characters need vertical space).

Effort

EFFORT: LOW Tailwind grid + per-pier size config. ~1 day.

4. Audience self-selector ("I am a…") ★★

A pre-content gate. The visitor declares who they are; the homepage then morphs to show them their pier first. You actually already have a thin version of this at /harbour/start.

Examples

  • Workday "what's your role?"
  • IBM persona-based home (Cloud)
  • Hubspot 2011 case study — the foundational pattern.
  • Instrumentl — separate pages for nonprofits vs. consultants.

How it would translate to harbour

Strengthen /harbour/start from afterthought to the default first-visit experience. New visitors land there; returning visitors land directly on /harbour. After picking a pier on /start, the user is taken to a focused page showing only their pier, with the other piers as small footer cards.

Why it works

Solves the "page is too long" problem by making the long page the secondary experience. PRME educators on May 28 land on /start, pick "i teach in higher-ed", and immediately see Pier B as a full-page experience rather than the third section down.

Why it might not

Adds friction (one extra click before content). Not all visitors fit cleanly into one persona. Mitigation: don't gate the main page — keep /harbour as the all-in-one entry. Just promote /start more prominently in the Hero.

Effort

EFFORT: LOW-MED The /start page exists; making it the first-visit default needs a cookie + redirect. Per-pier focused pages ~1 day per pier.

5. Pillar columns ("what we do" verticals) ★★

Three or four vertical columns side-by-side, each representing one product line. Like a Greek temple — same-height pillars, each labelled.

Examples

  • IDEO — pillared sections for main service offerings.
  • Stripe products page — pillars per product family.
  • Pentagram — pillared work categories, each visually distinct.
  • Notion "for every team" — pillars for engineering, design, product.

How it would translate to harbour

The three piers as three columns, side-by-side at desktop, stacked on mobile. Each column visually mimics a pier shape (taller-than-wide, with a "head" at top where the pier label lives, "body" where the cards live, "tip" extending downward into water). Drydock becomes a fourth column or a footer band.

Why it works

This is genuinely pier-shaped (tall vertical structures jutting from a baseline). It allows side-by-side comparison — a visitor can see Pier A, B, and C at a glance without scrolling.

Why it might not

Three columns of content at desktop means each has only ~33% width — Pier A's 4-5 apps would feel cramped. And on mobile it collapses back to a vertical list, losing the pier feel.

Effort

EFFORT: LOW CSS grid + responsive collapse. ~1 day.

6. Magazine / editorial section layout ★★

The NYT homepage approach: each "section" of the page is a self-contained editorial unit with its own headline, lead image, and supporting items. Visual variety comes from differently-styled sections.

Examples

  • NYT homepage — textbook reference.
  • The Atlantic — looser but same instinct.
  • The Verge — modernised with strong inter-section colour blocks.
  • The Pudding — editorial-driven section structure for data stories.

How it would translate to harbour

Each pier renders as a section with its own micro-design language: Pier A is "newspaper-classical" (serif headers, clean grid), Pier B is "academic journal" (numbered, footnoted feel), Pier C is "kids' magazine" (playful, illustrated, character-heavy).

Why it could work

Each pier becomes its own world without expensive custom illustration — typography and layout do the heavy lifting.

Why it might not

Risks looking incoherent if the styles are too different. Brand consistency matters more than maximally differentiated piers.

Effort

EFFORT: MEDIUM Multiple typography systems = more CSS + more design decisions. ~3 days.

7. Gaming launcher dashboards ★★

The Steam / Epic / Battle.net approach: a single dense screen showing what's available, what's installed, what's new, with filtering by category. Optimised for users who already know what they want.

Examples

  • Steam home page — left sidebar filter, central row-based content.
  • Epic Games Store — sparser, more curated, hero carousel + grid below.
  • Xbox Mode dashboard (Windows 11, April 2026) — controller-first, large tiles, aggregated library.
  • GOG Galaxy / Playnite — unified library across stores. "Many sources, one surface."

How it would translate to harbour

Add a filter bar at the top (All / Pier A / Pier B / Pier C / Drydock), keep the vertical structure below, but treat the page like a library.

Why it could work

PRME educators arriving via certification might think of this as "the harbour library" — a place to browse what's available.

Why it might not

Loses the metaphor. Piers exist to segment, not just filter. A unified library tells you everything is equivalent — which contradicts the audience-zoning rationale.

Effort

EFFORT: LOW Filter chips + JS visibility toggles. Half a day.

8. Tabbed / segmented control zones ★

A single content area with tabs or segmented controls that switch between pier views. Mobile-app navigation translated to web.

Examples

  • Apple Music tabs
  • Linear product page tabs
  • Figma Community

Why this is probably wrong for harbour

Tabs hide content. If a PRME educator lands and the default tab is Pier A (leadership), they might never realise Pier B exists for them. Tabs work when users come with intent; they fail when users come to discover.

Why it might still work

On mobile, tabs are sometimes the only way to fit four sections without infinite scroll.

9. Scrollytelling / narrative scroll ★

A long-form scroll-driven experience where the layout changes as you scroll.

Examples

  • NYT Snow Fall (2012) — the canonical example.
  • Pudding visual essays
  • National Geographic immersive features

How it would translate to harbour

The visitor scrolls and the harbour reveals itself — first sky, then water, then Pier A, then Pier B. Heavy use of position-sticky and scroll-triggered animations.

Why it probably shouldn't be the main pier IA

  • Slow to build and animate. Bad for return visitors who just want to launch creaseworks.
  • Accessibility — scroll-driven animation often fails screen readers and reduced-motion preferences.
  • Production cost is high (1-2 weeks of motion design + dev).

Effort

EFFORT: HIGH

10. 3D / WebGL "world" navigation ★

A literal 3D environment the visitor explores. The most aggressive interpretation of "the harbour."

Examples

  • Bruno Simon portfolio — drive a car around a 3D landscape.
  • Apple Vision Pro experience pages
  • Sketchfab
  • Three.js journey examples

Why this is probably wrong for harbour today

  • Months of production time.
  • Heavy bundle size — destroys the cache-headers win.
  • Accessibility-hostile by default.

When it might be right

Phase 3, post-launch, as an alternate entry point — /harbour/explore could be the 3D version while /harbour stays as the fast utility.

11. Hub-and-spoke / radial centre ★

A central "harbour" point with piers radiating outward as spokes.

Why this is probably wrong for harbour

Radial layouts don't fit responsive viewports. They look brilliant on a 1440px desktop and break on every other size.

12. Brutalist / typography-led ★

Strip out imagery; let oversized lowercase type carry the page. Type as architecture.

Examples

  • Pentagram — type-led, work case studies in giant title cards.
  • Are.na — minimal, typography-driven channel browsing.

How it would translate to harbour

"pier a — leadership" as 96-point lowercase Inter (or chunkier display face), with app names underneath at 24pt. Almost no imagery. Brand colour blocks separate piers.

Why it could work

Your brand voice is already strongly typographic — going hard on type is on-brand. Cheapest of all directions.

Why it might not

Doesn't actually solve the "feels like a vertical list" complaint — it just makes the list bigger.

Effort

EFFORT: VERY LOW Half a day of CSS.

13. Fog of war / exploration reveal ★★★ NEW

Areas of a map are initially dim or obscured; they "light up" as the visitor explores — scrolls, hovers, or otherwise interacts. Borrowed wholesale from tabletop RPGs and video games where progressive map reveal is part of the experience itself. This was your suggestion — Roll20's Explorer Mode applied to a website.

Examples — the originating mechanic

  • Roll20 Fog of War — the standard tabletop tool. GM manually reveals rooms as players enter them.
  • Roll20 Advanced Fog of War — dynamic version where each token's individual path is recorded and revealed automatically.
  • Roll20 Dynamic Lighting + Explorer Mode — once a token sees an area, it stays revealed in a dimmer sepia tone. "Been there, can remember it, but can't see what's happening there right now."
  • Crimson Desert — world map is almost entirely hidden until explored on foot or horseback. Discovery is the verb.

Examples — fog-of-war applied to web/UX

  • Fog of War web demo (React + HTML Canvas) — open-source web app that simulates the mechanic. Two canvas layers: map below, fog above; the cursor "uncovers" the map as it moves. Pattern is portable.
  • Animal Crossing: New Horizons — the island map starts fully visible BUT individual buildings/landmarks light up as you walk past them for the first time. Bridges the "exploration reveals content" gesture into a friendly tone (no sense of menace, just discovery).
  • Bruno Simon's 3D portfolio — sections of his portfolio are genuinely hidden until you drive your car into them. Most extreme version of the pattern on the web.

How it would translate to harbour

Combine with category 1 (illustrated map). Layered approach:

  • Layer 1 — the harbour at rest: top-down illustrated harbour SVG fills the hero. At first paint, the entire scene is rendered in a muted, washed-out palette. The three piers are visible as silhouettes but dim — you can see there are docks but not what's on them.
  • Layer 2 — exploration: as the visitor scrolls (or, on a static page, as they hover over a pier), the section "lights up" — the pier's tone warms, the cards on it become visible and crisp, and the labels become legible. The cast parade between Pier B and Pier C becomes the literal animation point where things move.
  • Layer 3 — memory: once a pier has been "visited" (scrolled into view, or clicked), it stays lit even when you scroll away. Mirrors Roll20's Explorer Mode — "I've been here before, I remember." Stored client-side in localStorage so returning visitors see their previous path.
  • Pier C as the lighthouse: Pier C ("opening end of june") could literally be the part of the harbour with a working lighthouse — even when dim, the light sweeps across the harbour, periodically illuminating the other piers in brief flashes. Subtle teaser that draws the eye toward what's coming.
  • Drydock as off-water: the drydock is on land, behind the piers. Rendered in even more muted tones. Each drydock app "lights up" individually as you scroll into that section, but doesn't get the warmth of the working piers — emphasising "not yet" rather than "for you."

Why it's the most powerful direction

  • The metaphor stops being decorative and becomes mechanical. Right now "pier" is a word in a header; with this, a pier is a thing you literally walk down. The page rewards exploration with content.
  • Encourages the right behaviour. A PRME educator landing on May 28 sees the whole harbour dim. They scroll, and Pier B lights up specifically for them — they understand instantly "this is mine" without an explicit audience selector.
  • Returning visitors get continuity. The lit-up state persists. A returning visitor sees what they've already explored and what they haven't. It builds a relationship with the page across visits.
  • It's playable. The harbour is winded.vertigo's "playful learning ecosystem" — making the landing itself a small piece of play is on-brand in a way no other category here is.

Why it might not work — the accessibility and mobile concerns you flagged

These are real and need explicit answers:

  • Screen readers: a screen reader doesn't "scroll past" a pier — it reads the DOM. So the content must be fully present in the DOM at first paint, with the fog effect being purely visual via CSS opacity/filter. Screen-reader users get the full content as a linear document; visual users get the exploration overlay. The fog is decoration, never content.
  • Keyboard navigation: Tab-key traversal needs to "reveal" piers as a side-effect of focus moving into them. Implement focus-triggered reveal in addition to scroll/hover. Keyboard users still get the discovery feeling.
  • Reduced-motion preferences: the page must respect prefers-reduced-motion. Users with that setting see the full harbour lit from the start — no fog effect at all. Same content, no animation.
  • Mobile touchscreens: hover doesn't exist. On mobile, the reveal happens purely on scroll-into-view (which is the dominant interaction on mobile anyway). No worse than the current behaviour.
  • Slow connections: the SVG illustration could be heavy. Mitigation: ship a low-res silhouette first (~5KB), then progressively enhance with the full illustration. Or do the entire effect with CSS filters on a single SVG, which loads in one request.
  • UDL (Universal Design for Learning): the underlying content is identical regardless of the fog state. A user who never moves their mouse still gets every word, every card, every link — just with all sections dimmer. That's UDL-compliant: equivalent content, multiple modes of access.

Effort

EFFORT: HIGH Needs:

  1. The illustrated harbour SVG (same dependency as category 1 — see graphics section).
  2. A small JavaScript module (~150 lines) that tracks which piers have been "visited" via IntersectionObserver, persists state in localStorage, and toggles a CSS class on each pier section.
  3. CSS for the dimmed state, the lit state, the transition between them, and the "explored but currently off-screen" Roll20-style memory state.
  4. Reduced-motion + screen-reader fallback behaviour.

Realistic timeline: 5-8 working days end-to-end if we have the SVG already. Most of that is illustration + interaction polish, not the JS itself.

The compromise version (if 5-8 days is too long)

Ship the fog-of-war without the illustrated map: use the current boardwalk rails (category 2) but apply the dimming/lighting effect to each rail individually. Pier A starts dim; scroll into it and the boardwalk lights up. Cheaper graphics (just CSS), same psychological hook. About 2-3 days. Less impressive than the full map version, but the mechanic is preserved and that's where the magic lives.

My synthesised recommendation

The fog-of-war suggestion changes my recommendation. Two-phase plan:

Phase 1 — ship for May 28 (10 days): Boardwalk rails (category 2) + tonal backgrounds per pier (subset of category 3) + the lightweight fog-of-war effect applied to each rail (category 13 compromise version). Each pier starts dim; scrolling into it lights it up; once visited, it stays lit.

Costs ~3-4 days of work. Lands the metaphor without requiring custom illustration.

Phase 2 — ship mid-June (post-launch): Commission the illustrated harbour map. Replace the textual hero with the SVG. The full fog-of-war effect then operates on the map itself — piers literally light up as visitors explore. This is the version that wins design awards.

Costs the illustrator's fee (£400-£1,500) and ~1 week of dev work to integrate.

This sequencing is honest about the May 28 timeline: a fog-of-war over CSS boardwalks is ~80% as evocative as the full map version, ships in time, and the second phase upgrades the experience without throwing away the first phase's work.

Graphics we'd need to create

For phase 1 (boardwalks + light fog-of-war), you need:

  1. Plank texture (CSS-only) — a linear-gradient SVG that creates wood-grain bands. ~30 lines of CSS, no asset needed.
  2. Wave divider (SVG) — one reusable SVG of a stylised wave shape used between piers. ~40 lines of SVG. I can write this inline.
  3. Dim/lit state CSS — `filter: saturate(.3) brightness(.7)` for dim, full saturation for lit. Pure CSS.
  4. Optional: pier-end marker (SVG) — a small lighthouse, buoy, or flag at the "end" of each boardwalk. You pick; I draw.

For phase 2 (illustrated map), you additionally need:

  1. Top-down harbour SVG — three piers visible, water surrounding, a sandy/wooded drydock zone. Roughly 2400×1200px at 2x, or scalable SVG. 3-7 days of illustrator work, or 1-2 days of stock-isometric composition if we DIY. I'd lean toward commissioning — AI-generated illustrations rarely match a specific brand palette without significant cleanup.

Decision questions for you

  1. Phase 1 direction: boardwalks + light fog-of-war (my pick), or boardwalks alone, or skip straight to commissioning the illustrated map and ship the bigger version even if it slips past May 28?
  2. If boardwalks: pier label parallel to the planks (more pier-like, harder to read) or above as a section header (safer)?
  3. Pier-end marker: lighthouse (guidance), buoy (playful), flag (territory), or none?
  4. Per-pier backgrounds: strong tonal shift (distinct zones, risks fragmenting brand) or subtle (unified, risks reading as undifferentiated)?
  5. Make /start the first-visit default? PRME educators land on /start, pick "I teach in higher-ed", and immediately see Pier B as a focused page.
  6. Commission an illustrator for phase 5? If yes, I'll write the brief and source illustrators. If no, we either DIY with stock isometrics or skip the map entirely.
  7. For the fog-of-war: how aggressive should "dim" be? Options: heavily desaturated (Roll20-style, almost monochrome) → subtly faded (just a hint of dimness). Aggressive is more dramatic but risks first-time visitors thinking the page is broken.
