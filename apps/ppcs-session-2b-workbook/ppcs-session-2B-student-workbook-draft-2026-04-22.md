# the three intelligences

*a self-assessment and practice workbook for business students*

*draft · for faculty review before final formatting · 22 April 2026*

---

## what this is

three intelligences quietly shape how well you lead, decide, and work with others in business. most courses train one of them and assume the other two. this workbook is a way to see which you already have strong, which you're still building, and what to practise day-to-day to grow the ones that are thinner.

the three are:
- **creative intelligence** — how well you generate options and hold uncertainty.
- **emotional intelligence** — how well you notice and manage what you're feeling.
- **collaboration** — how well you think together with people who see things differently.

rate yourself honestly on nine statements. plot your spider web. read the shape. pick your two weakest axes and practise from those sections across the next four weeks. come back and re-rate at the end of the semester.

no one sees your answers unless you want them to.

## meet the three intelligences

now that you've mapped yourself, the names:

**creative intelligence** is your ability to generate more than one option, hold a problem in uncertainty, and see the same question from a different angle. it is not artistic talent. it is how novel thinking gets made. see Brown (2019).

**emotional intelligence** is your ability to notice what you're feeling, manage it under pressure, and read what others are feeling. recent neuroscience shows emotion and cognition are one integrated system — you cannot decide well without it. see Goleman (2018) and Bradberry & Greaves (2009).

**collaboration** is your ability to listen to views you disagree with, change your mind when a peer is right, and disagree without making it personal. it is how groups get smarter than individuals. see Scharmer (2016).

one body of research suggests EI alone accounts for 58% of job performance across every job type (Bradberry & Greaves, 2009). the other two are similarly load-bearing — and all three are developable through practice.

## creative intelligence — practices

### personal practices (solo, outside class)

1. **the three-options rule.** for any small decision today — what to eat, what to watch, how to spend 30 min — list three options before choosing. not two. three.
2. **opposite-day prompt.** once a day, ask: *what would the opposite of the obvious answer look like?* out loud, in a note, in conversation.
3. **two-answer habit.** when someone asks your opinion, give two. *I could see it going this way or that way.*
4. **ten-minute silent walk.** no phone, no podcast, no music. let the mind go where it goes. notice when it reaches for a conclusion.
5. **the 24-hour rule.** before making any non-urgent decision, wait 24 hours. note what your first instinct was and whether it changed.
6. **constraint remix.** once a week, redo a task you already did, but with one constraint removed or added. the email you wrote yesterday, but in three sentences. the route you walked, but the long way.
7. **borrowed-lens practice.** pick one person whose thinking you admire — how would they approach the problem you're stuck on?
8. **cross-domain reading.** one article or chapter per week from a field you know nothing about. no purpose beyond exposure.
9. **whiteboard-before-search.** before googling an answer, sketch out what you think it might be. then search.
10. **the "what if" question.** when you're stuck, ask: *what if the opposite were true? what if there were no budget? what if there were no deadline?*
11. **the stranger test.** explain your problem to someone outside your field in plain language. notice what they ask.
12. **drawing, not writing.** when you can't figure something out in words, draw it — even badly.

### school / classroom practices

1. **before-reading hook.** open any case or reading with one question: *what's the non-obvious angle here?* write it before you read.
2. **group-work rule.** in any group discussion, propose at least two options, never one. make it your reputation.
3. **before-convergence.** when a group starts agreeing quickly, ask: *what are we missing?*
4. **the analogy move.** in case analysis, ask: *what other industry or context does this remind me of?* borrow the framework from there.
5. **steelman the wrong answer.** for every case, write a one-paragraph argument for the option you don't think is right. notice what it makes visible.
6. **the professor's blind spot.** pick one thing the professor seems to take for granted. write a note to yourself: *what if this isn't true?*
7. **reframe the question.** before answering a discussion question, rewrite it. what is it actually asking? what is it not asking?
8. **present what you didn't pick.** in a pitch or proposal, include one slide explaining the path you considered and rejected. most presenters skip this. it raises the quality of every other slide.
9. **the three-angle case brief.** when you brief a case, write three different openings — *this is a strategy problem · this is a people problem · this is a finance problem.* pick the strongest.
10. **one-question syllabus.** each week, pick one big question from your course that doesn't have a clean answer. hold it with you. return to it.
11. **devil's advocate rotation.** in your study group, rotate who plays devil's advocate each session. make it structural, not personal.
12. **what would you have done?** after every case, before the debrief, write one paragraph: *what would I have done if I were in charge?* compare with the actual outcome.

## emotional intelligence — practices

### personal practices (solo, outside class)

1. **weather check, 3x a day.** phone alarms at 11am, 3pm, 8pm. at each alarm, pause 30 seconds: *what am I feeling right now — in one word?* no analysis, just name.
2. **one-word day log.** before bed, write one word in your notes app for the day you just had. after a week, read them back.
3. **two-minute body scan on waking.** before your phone, notice where in your body you feel something — tight shoulders, clenched jaw, loose chest. name it, don't fix it.
4. **4-7-8 breathing.** breathe in 4 sec, hold 7, out 8. three rounds before anything stressful.
5. **the gap between trigger and action.** when something bothers you, count to 10 before responding. notice how the intensity changes.
6. **trigger journal.** keep a note in your phone: things that triggered a strong reaction this week. after a month, look for patterns.
7. **the feeling-behind-the-feeling.** when you feel angry, ask: *what am I actually afraid of? what am I sad about?* anger often masks something else.
8. **five-minute walk after a hard conversation.** no phone. let the feeling move through you before you move to the next thing.
9. **the compliment intake.** when someone compliments you, don't deflect. say *thank you* and notice the feeling that comes up.
10. **one morning page.** one page of writing, by hand, first thing. nothing is wrong with anything you write.
11. **sleep check.** when you feel emotionally reactive, ask: *how did I sleep last night?* a huge amount of emotional reactivity is physical.
12. **the "this is temporary" note.** when you feel a big emotion, write on paper: *this feeling will end.* it sounds trite; it helps.

### school / classroom practices

1. **pre-class 30-second check-in.** before walking into a class you find difficult, pause: *what am I bringing to this room today?* one word.
2. **write-before-respond.** when you feel defensive in a class discussion, write the word for what you're feeling in your notebook before you respond.
3. **after-class walk.** after a tough class, take a 5-min walk alone before going to your next thing. notice what shifted.
4. **cold-call breath.** before you answer a cold-call, take one audible breath. it slows the room down; it slows you down.
5. **partner weather report.** before starting a group project meeting, each person says one word about how they're arriving. no explanation required.
6. **the private pre-feedback note.** before giving feedback to a teammate, write a private note: *what do I want this person to feel as they read this?*
7. **the body check after feedback.** after delivering tough feedback, notice your body. were you compressed or open? your body tells you if it landed well.
8. **the two-column listening.** during an emotional group meeting, take notes in two columns — what was said on the left, how it felt on the right.
9. **the "what's the smallest thing" question.** when you feel overwhelmed before an exam or deadline, ask: *what is the smallest thing I can do in 10 min?* then do it.
10. **the public name.** when someone's upset in a group and no one's saying it, be the person who names it. *I notice the energy shifted.*
11. **the private audit after group work.** after a group project, rate yourself 1–5 on how much you listened vs. pushed. note one thing you'd do differently.
12. **the "I'm going to be honest" pause.** before you say something hard, say out loud: *I want to be honest with you about something.* it signals you're about to be vulnerable; it softens the other person.

## collaboration — practices

### personal practices (solo, outside class)

1. **the rule of three.** before you speak in any conversation, name three things the other person said.
2. **one-source-per-week.** read one article from a source you disagree with per week. engage with the strongest version of the argument, not the weakest.
3. **the mind-change log.** note in your phone: times you changed your position and why. after a month, review.
4. **play devil's advocate to yourself.** once a week, write a paragraph arguing against your strongest view. feel the discomfort. notice what it teaches.
5. **the silent friend.** in any group conversation, notice who isn't speaking. text them afterward to ask what they thought.
6. **the interview mode.** once a week, spend a meal asking someone questions only. no opinions, no stories of your own. just questions.
7. **reading-before-responding.** when you get a text or email that lands wrong, read it three times before you respond. meaning usually shifts.
8. **the "tell me more" default.** when someone shares something you disagree with, say *tell me more* instead of countering. see what shows up.
9. **the 24-hour response rule.** when something online makes you want to argue, wait 24 hours. 95% of the time you won't want to.
10. **call-instead-of-text.** for any conversation that's getting tense in text, call. tone carries more than words.
11. **write their argument better than they did.** before disagreeing with someone, write their position more clearly than they did. notice what you had to add.
12. **the "what would change my mind" question.** for every strong view you hold, identify in advance: *what evidence would change my mind?* write it down.

### school / classroom practices

1. **speak once, then listen.** in any group discussion, make a rule with yourself: you speak once, then listen until everyone has spoken once. *then* you can speak again.
2. **steelman your opponent.** before any debate or case argument, write the opposing position more strongly than the people who hold it do. argue yours only after.
3. **the weekly mind-change note.** keep a note each week: *what did I change my mind about in class this week, and why?* if nothing, that's data.
4. **partner rotations.** in every group project, try to partner with someone you haven't worked with before. the friction is the point.
5. **the one-question contribution.** in any class where you don't feel you have a strong take, contribute one question instead. questions count.
6. **the "I'm not sure" declaration.** in class discussion, say *I'm not sure* out loud at least once per week. it's the most undervalued phrase in a classroom.
7. **the late-talker spotlight.** in group work, notice who hasn't spoken. ask them directly: *what's your take?* don't let anyone hide.
8. **credit-sharing habit.** when you build on someone else's idea, name them. *to build on what Sara said...* — make it audible.
9. **the disagreement protocol.** when you disagree in class, structure it: *I hear you saying X. I see it differently because Y. here's what would change my mind.*
10. **office hours for the uncomfortable.** go to office hours specifically for the class or concept you find most confusing. sit with the discomfort rather than dodging it.
11. **the post-class three.** after every class, name three things — one you agreed with, one you disagreed with, one that surprised you.
12. **the team retro.** at the end of every group project, do a 20-min retro: what worked, what didn't, what would we do differently. make it a default, not an exception.

## using this workbook over a semester

**week 1.** rate yourself. plot your web. identify your two weakest axes.

**weeks 2–5.** pick three personal practices and three school practices from your two weakest axes. the practices that feel slightly uncomfortable are the ones working. track them in a note on your phone — which ones you tried, which ones stuck.

**week 6.** mid-semester re-rate. plot a new web in a different colour. compare.

**weeks 7–14.** rotate practices. the ones that became habit are done — pick new ones. the ones that didn't stick, drop them. replace with three new ones.

**week 15.** final re-rate. plot your third web. note which axes shifted and which didn't. the ones that didn't may need a different kind of practice than this workbook offers — talk to your professor, a counsellor, a peer.

**rule of thumb.** one axis moves noticeably in a semester. two if you're disciplined. three means you weren't honest on the first rating.
