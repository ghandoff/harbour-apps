the three intelligences

a self-assessment and practice workbook

this is a self-assessment and practice workbook built around three forms of intelligence that matter most in the work of responsible management: the ability to think creatively across difference, to navigate your own emotions and those of others, and to collaborate across genuine disagreement. it is not a test and it has no correct score. what it gives you is a picture of where you are now, a set of practices grounded in that picture, and a way to track how that picture changes over a semester.

self-assessment

rate each statement from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). be honest — there are no correct scores.

creative intelligence

the capacity to see more than one way through a problem, to sit with ambiguity long enough for something new to surface, and to look at a familiar situation from a perspective you have not tried yet. it is not the same as artistic talent. it is a cognitive habit that can be practised.

when I face a problem, I usually come up with more than one way to solve it.

I'm comfortable not knowing the answer right away.

when I'm stuck, I can look at the same problem from a completely different angle.

emotional intelligence

the ability to name what you are feeling in the moment it happens, to stay functional when you are under pressure, and to read what others are experiencing even when they have not said so. it is the intelligence that sits beneath all the others.

I can name what I'm feeling when I feel it — not hours later.

when I'm stressed, I can still think clearly.

I can usually tell when someone is upset even if they haven't said so.

collaboration intelligence

the willingness to listen to a view you disagree with as if it might be right, to let a better argument actually change your position, and to push back on an idea without making the disagreement personal. it is the intelligence that makes groups better than individuals.

I listen to views I disagree with with real curiosity, not just to respond.

I've changed my mind because a peer's argument was better than mine.

I can disagree with someone without making it personal.

semester tracker

return to this workbook at the start, middle, and end of your semester. each time you come back, re-rate yourself on the nine statements. your web will shift — some axes will grow as you practise deliberately, others will surface as blind spots you had not noticed.

the radar keeps a history of your last three webs so you can see the direction of change, not just your current position. the practice plan updates with each re-rating, pulling from whichever intelligences you rated lowest.

the goal is not a perfect score. it is an honest account of where you are, and a set of practical steps toward where you want to be.

note to your faculty

this workbook was completed as part of my programme's engagement with the three intelligences framework. the self-assessment reflects my own honest rating of nine statements across creative, emotional, and collaborative intelligence. the practice plan shown here was generated from my two lowest-rated axes and draws from a pool of 72 evidence-informed practices. I have used the semester tracker to revisit my ratings and can show how my radar has changed across the term. the sources cited in the closing section ground the framework in peer-reviewed research. I am happy to discuss my ratings and the practices I chose.
