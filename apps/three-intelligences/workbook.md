# the three intelligences — a self-assessment and practice workbook

## what this is

this is a self-assessment and practice workbook built around three forms of intelligence that matter most in the work of responsible management: the ability to think creatively across difference, to navigate your own emotions and those of others, and to collaborate across genuine disagreement. it is not a test and it has no correct score. what it gives you is a picture of where you are now, a set of practices grounded in that picture, and a way to track how that picture changes over a semester.

## meet the three intelligences

**creative intelligence** is the capacity to see more than one way through a problem, to sit with ambiguity long enough for something new to surface, and to look at a familiar situation from a perspective you have not tried yet. it is not the same as artistic talent. it is a cognitive habit that can be practised.

**emotional intelligence** is the ability to name what you are feeling in the moment it happens, to stay functional when you are under pressure, and to read what others are experiencing even when they have not said so. it is the intelligence that sits beneath all the others.

**collaboration intelligence** is the willingness to listen to a view you disagree with as if it might be right, to let a better argument actually change your position, and to push back on an idea without making the disagreement personal. it is the intelligence that makes groups better than individuals.

## the nine statements

q1 (creative · generate alternatives): when I face a problem, I usually come up with more than one way to solve it.
q2 (creative · tolerate ambiguity): I'm comfortable not knowing the answer right away.
q3 (creative · reframe): when I'm stuck, I can look at the same problem from a completely different angle.
q4 (emotional · self-awareness): I can name what I'm feeling when I feel it — not hours later.
q5 (emotional · self-regulation): when I'm stressed, I can still think clearly.
q6 (emotional · empathy): I can usually tell when someone is upset even if they haven't said so.
q7 (collaboration · active listening): I listen to views I disagree with with real curiosity, not just to respond.
q8 (collaboration · persuadable): I've changed my mind because a peer's argument was better than mine.
q9 (collaboration · productive challenge): I can disagree with someone without making it personal.

## practices

### creative intelligence — personal practices

#### cr-p-1 divergence journal
keep a daily list of alternative solutions to problems you encounter. aim for five options before choosing one.

#### cr-p-2 constraint creation
impose an artificial limit on your next project — no budget, no internet, one colour only — and notice how limits force originality.

#### cr-p-3 reverse the brief
take a problem you are working on and rewrite it from the opposite direction. what would anti-success look like?

#### cr-p-4 input expansion
spend one hour each week consuming something completely outside your field — a documentary genre, a craft, a sport — and write down what it changes in your thinking.

#### cr-p-5 idea archaeology
dig up an abandoned idea from six months ago and ask why you stopped. see if the obstacle still exists.

#### cr-p-6 ambiguity sit
pick one open question in your work and deliberately resist answering it for 48 hours. observe what surfaces.

#### cr-p-7 borrowed lens
pick a different profession — chef, architect, social worker — and describe your current problem using their vocabulary.

#### cr-p-8 prototype fast
take your next idea and make a rough physical or digital version within two hours. speed matters more than quality.

#### cr-p-9 yes-and solo
take your own first idea and build three extensions to it using only yes-and logic — no negation allowed.

#### cr-p-10 failure portfolio
collect three things you tried that did not work. write one sentence on what each one taught you.

#### cr-p-11 question swarm
take a decision you need to make and generate 20 questions about it before considering any answers.

#### cr-p-12 metaphor mapping
describe your current project using a completely different domain — cooking, ecology, music. notice what the metaphor reveals.

### creative intelligence — school practices

#### cr-s-1 facilitated alternatives
in your next class discussion, commit to proposing at least two different framings before settling on one.

#### cr-s-2 assignment reframe
when you receive a brief, write your own version of the assignment question before reading the instructions fully. compare the two.

#### cr-s-3 cross-cohort lens
interview a student from a different programme about how they would approach your current project. bring one of their ideas into your work.

#### cr-s-4 studio critique
arrange a five-minute informal critique of your work-in-progress with two peers who did not work on it. take notes without defending.

#### cr-s-5 speculative presentation
create a slide deck for an idea that probably will not work. present it as if it will.

#### cr-s-6 rapid ideation sprint
in your next team meeting, set a five-minute timer and generate as many ideas as possible before evaluating any of them.

#### cr-s-7 uncertainty map
draw a visual map of everything you do not yet know about your current assignment. use it as a research guide.

#### cr-s-8 prototype gallery
make three rough versions of the same deliverable in different formats — visual, written, diagrammatic — and compare what each one reveals.

#### cr-s-9 borrowed framework
apply a framework from a module you have already completed to your current module's problem. write up what transfers and what does not.

#### cr-s-10 reverse peer review
give a peer feedback on what you would change, then ask them to tell you what was already strong. track the ratio.

#### cr-s-11 creative brief swap
exchange assignment briefs with a peer from a different cohort. draft a response to theirs; share the drafts.

#### cr-s-12 what-if seminar
bring one what-if-the-opposite-were-true question to your next seminar and see how far the class can take it.

### emotional intelligence — personal practices

#### em-p-1 real-time naming
when you notice a shift in your mood, pause and name it specifically. not "bad" but "frustrated because I feel unseen".

#### em-p-2 body scan anchor
once a day, spend two minutes checking in with physical sensations before labelling your emotional state. start with the chest, then shoulders, then jaw.

#### em-p-3 trigger mapping
keep a note of three recurring situations that reliably shift your emotional state. look for patterns across a fortnight.

#### em-p-4 emotional lag check
at the end of the day, ask: was there a moment when I named what I felt hours after it happened? what slowed the recognition?

#### em-p-5 stress inventory
when stress peaks, write down what specifically is driving it. separate the controllable from the uncontrollable.

#### em-p-6 one-breath rule
when you feel reactive in conversation, commit to one breath before responding. track how often you use it.

#### em-p-7 clarity window
identify the hour in your day when you think most clearly. protect it for tasks that require calm judgment.

#### em-p-8 emotion palette
build a personal vocabulary of 20 emotional states, including shades beyond angry, sad, happy. use them in your journalling.

#### em-p-9 self-compassion prompt
when something goes wrong, write what you would say to a close friend in the same situation. notice if it differs from how you speak to yourself.

#### em-p-10 emotional forecast
at the start of each day, write a short prediction of when and why you might find it hard to stay regulated. see how accurate you are.

#### em-p-11 recovery ritual
design a five-minute routine you use specifically to return to calm after a difficult interaction. practise it enough to make it automatic.

#### em-p-12 distraction audit
when you avoid a task, pause and name the emotion behind the avoidance. track what you are most often avoiding and why.

### emotional intelligence — school practices

#### em-s-1 pre-meeting check-in
before any group session, take 60 seconds to notice your current state. share it briefly with your team using one word.

#### em-s-2 emotional framing
when receiving feedback, write down what you felt immediately, then re-read the feedback 24 hours later. compare your two readings.

#### em-s-3 lecture self-regulation
when your attention drifts in a lecture or seminar, note the moment and what pulled you away. look for patterns across three weeks.

#### em-s-4 assessment emotion log
for each major piece of work, write a short note about how you felt submitting it and why. build a library of these over the semester.

#### em-s-5 peer calibration
find out how a peer whose judgment you respect would describe their emotional experience of the same module. notice what is shared and what is different.

#### em-s-6 stress-to-strategy map
for each assessment deadline, write a brief plan for how you will manage your emotional state in the 48 hours before submission.

#### em-s-7 feedback receipt protocol
design a personal two-step ritual for receiving written feedback: read once without annotating, then re-read with a pen.

#### em-s-8 seminar regulation
when a seminar discussion triggers a strong reaction, choose to listen for two full minutes before contributing. track how it changes your input.

#### em-s-9 visibility practice
in a class where you normally stay quiet, commit to one contribution per session for three weeks. note how it feels each time.

#### em-s-10 module transition
between modules, write a short paragraph describing how you feel leaving one subject and entering another. notice what carries over.

#### em-s-11 conflict note
when a disagreement arises in a team project, write a private account of your emotional experience of it within two hours. look for your patterns.

#### em-s-12 wellbeing check-in
at the end of each teaching week, rate your sense of groundedness out of five and note one thing that anchored it. look for trends.

### collaboration intelligence — personal practices

#### co-p-1 listen longer
in your next three conversations, commit to making no contribution until the other person has fully finished their thought and you have paused for two seconds.

#### co-p-2 persuasion log
keep a record of three times this month that someone changed your mind. write what the argument was and what made it land.

#### co-p-3 disagreement style audit
after a conflict or disagreement, write down what you did, what you wish you had done differently, and what the other person might have experienced.

#### co-p-4 question-first habit
in conversations where you feel an urge to give your opinion, convert your first two responses into questions instead.

#### co-p-5 role reversal
take a position you disagreed with recently and write the strongest version of the argument for it. share it with someone who holds that position and ask if you got it right.

#### co-p-6 contribution inventory
for each team project, track privately whether your contributions have been additive — building on others — or substitutive — replacing others' ideas. aim for a higher additive ratio.

#### co-p-7 dissent practice
identify a team decision you agreed with publicly but privately questioned. draft the dissenting argument and ask yourself whether it deserved airtime.

#### co-p-8 silence appreciation
in your next group setting, identify the quietest person and ask them a direct question. reflect on what emerged.

#### co-p-9 feedback request
ask three people who work with you closely to describe your collaboration style in three words. compare their words with your self-perception.

#### co-p-10 repair practice
when a working relationship has been strained, initiate a brief, direct conversation about what happened. keep it focused on the work, not the person.

#### co-p-11 assumption check
before a difficult conversation, write down three things you assume the other person thinks or wants. check each assumption explicitly during the conversation.

#### co-p-12 gratitude specific
once a week, tell a collaborator specifically what they contributed that made the work better. avoid generic praise.

### collaboration intelligence — school practices

#### co-s-1 active reading partnership
share reading notes from a lecture or seminar with a peer before the next session. identify one thing you interpreted differently.

#### co-s-2 structured challenge
in your next group project meeting, assign one person the role of productive challenger for 15 minutes. rotate this role across the project.

#### co-s-3 presentation pre-test
rehearse your group presentation in front of one person outside your group. ask them to identify one thing that confused them.

#### co-s-4 contribution mapping
after completing a group project, draw a visual map showing what each member contributed. share and compare maps as a debrief.

#### co-s-5 negotiated roles
before starting a group assignment, spend ten minutes explicitly negotiating who does what and why. revisit the agreement at the midpoint.

#### co-s-6 peer interview
before a significant group project, interview each team member for five minutes about how they work best. use this to shape the team's process.

#### co-s-7 dissent protocol
agree as a group that any member can call a pause at any point to raise a concern without judgment. use it at least once.

#### co-s-8 feedback round
at the end of each major team task, give every member one specific piece of written feedback on their collaboration — not their output. read and discuss.

#### co-s-9 argument quality check
after a group decision, revisit the reasoning that led to it. could you all articulate the three strongest objections to the choice you made?

#### co-s-10 cross-group debrief
after a project, speak with a member of a different group about what worked in their process. bring one idea back to your group.

#### co-s-11 conflict resolution protocol
agree as a team, at the start of the project, on how you will handle disagreements. write it down. test it.

#### co-s-12 shared retrospective
at the end of each semester, write a one-page reflection on how your collaboration skills have changed and where the evidence for that change lives.

## using this workbook over a semester

return to this workbook at the start, middle, and end of your semester. each time you come back, re-rate yourself on the nine statements. your web will shift — some axes will grow as you practise deliberately, others will surface as blind spots you had not noticed. the radar keeps a history of your last three webs so you can see the direction of change, not just your current position. the practice plan updates with each re-rating, pulling from whichever intelligences you rated lowest. the goal is not a perfect score. it is an honest account of where you are, and a set of practical steps toward where you want to be.
